Exercise 2

Use the function mcmc_areas() to plot the smoothed posterior distribution for λ with a 90% Highest Posterior Density region. Changing the prob parameter in mcmc_areas() will change the amount of probability mass that is highlighted. The highlighted region will begin in regions of highest density, and will move towards regions of lower density as prob gets larger.

The line in the plot represents the posterior mean, and the shaded area represents 90% of the posterior density. How does the posterior mean for λ compare to the sample mean? Are they similar or not? Why are they different?

mcmc_areas(lambda_draws, prob = 0.9)

Answer: From the PPC results below, we could notice that in hurdle model a random variable is modelled using two parts, the first which is the probability of attaining value 0, and the second part models the probability of the non-zero values. The use of hurdle models are often motivated by an excess of zeroes in the data, that is not sufficiently accounted for in more standard statistical models.

Exercise 8: Why are PPCs important?

PPCs allow you to compare observed data with data simulated from your model.Graphical PPCs can help in identifying specific ways in which your model might not be a good fit for the data, and it could help you choose among models.
